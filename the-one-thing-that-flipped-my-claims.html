The One Thing That Flipped My Claims Overnight Wasn't Arguing Harder

May 28, 2026  ·  Oscar A. Hoyt IV, Aequus Claims
Oscar A. Hoyt IV
Founder, Aequus Claims  ·  I-CAR Gold Appraiser  ·  TX Licensed Adjuster  ·  35+ Years Industry

I opened my shop in 2014. I built everything working a day job during the day and building the business at night and on weekends. Long hours, no days off. My wife was running her own business while holding down the fort with our boys. She encouraged me to take the leap, even when she had moments of doubt. Her love and support never wavered.

I kept the doors open working on timeless C10 trucks until collision work began rolling in. I busted my tail to deliver quality repairs. Early on, out of ignorance, I often compromised under pressure from insurers — agreeing to aftermarket or lower-value parts even though I knew it wasn't right.

The Repair That Changed Everything

Then came a silver early-2000s Chevy Equinox. Straightforward front-end collision. Just bolt-on parts, nothing exotic. The insurer insisted on aftermarket components. The parts looked okay on the shelf — but once installed they fit like they were designed for this car's fourth cousin, twice removed. The owner took one quick look. A blink lasts longer than it took him to say: "Nope. Not happening."

I understood completely. The gaps and poor alignment were obvious. I apologized, pulled everything off, sent detailed photos to the adjuster, and fought for OE parts. Surprisingly, they approved the full switch. The aftermarket pieces weren't returnable, but the insurer paid. Once the car was finished correctly, the owner was thrilled. Reputation took a hit, but we salvaged it. How you handle mistakes often matters more than never making them.

That day taught me a hard lesson. I stopped compromising and committed to OE procedures and proper repairs. My repair severity rose. I became "the problem shop." And I wore it as a badge of honor.

The Bigger Problem: Losing Customers Before the Fight Even Started

But I still had one major problem: even when I did the right thing, many customers remained uneasy and tended to side with the insurance company. Why? Because insurers were telling them I was over-repairing or just trying to inflate the cost. Denying my claims was one thing. Disparaging me behind my back to my own clients felt personal.

Little do they know — adversity is my drug of choice. This was war.

The one thing that changed that dynamic was learning to explain the full repair cost and the "why" behind every line item to the customer before I ever sent the estimate to the insurer.

The Google Drive System That Turned the Tables

I started sharing a Google Drive folder link with every new client right after intake. Inside the folder were clear subfolders:

  • Client Need-to-Know — CAPA decertification lists, paint documents, definitions, how to file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance, images of aftermarket parts not fitting, safety notices, RTA information, and more.
  • Damage Photos & Video — A short 1–2 minute video walking around the vehicle, pointing out the damage and explaining what was required to fix it properly.
  • Procedures — OEM documents numbered and referenced directly to the estimate lines.
  • Estimates, Invoices & Communications

I sent the link — plus the damage video — to the customer before any discussion with the insurer. Then I followed up with a call to make sure the vehicle owner understood and approved the repairs, and a follow-up email confirming what we discussed.

The Results Were Dramatic

When the adjuster called the client to say I was over-repairing or being unreasonable, the customer was already armed with facts, photos, and procedures. I started getting calls from clients saying, "You won't believe what the adjuster just tried to tell me." The tables turned. Customers stopped trusting the insurer's spin because they had the real information first.

This didn't win every claim. But it set up the tough ones for stronger negotiations, RTA success, or court if it ever went that far.
