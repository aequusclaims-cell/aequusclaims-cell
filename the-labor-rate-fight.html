Every shop owner in the collision industry knows the number. They know what insurance is paying. They know what they need to charge. And they know the gap between those two numbers is real, documented, and getting wider every year. The question has never been whether the gap exists. The question — the one nobody wants to say out loud — is what does it actually cost you to fight it?

The Four Ways Insurance Actually Pays Your Door Rate

In 35 years in this industry, including a decade owning an OE-certified shop, I have seen a shop's true door rate get paid exactly four ways. It helps to be honest about all of them.

One: The quality carrier pays it. Some carriers — a shrinking number — still negotiate in good faith. Their adjusters have authority and use it. These relationships exist. They are not the norm.

Two: The adjuster lets it slide. Sometimes an adjuster is overloaded, undertrained, or simply does not push back on a well-documented file. This is not a strategy. It is luck, and you cannot build a business on luck.

Three: The client gets reimbursed after fighting. The vehicle owner goes to bat for their shop. They file complaints with the state insurance department. They push back. They get some or all of the difference — eventually. This took time, energy, and a client willing to go to war on your behalf. Most are not.

Four: The appraisal clause is invoked. Right to Appraisal is a legitimate and powerful tool. It is also heavily contested, frequently delayed, and often results in a umpire decision that splits the difference. Even a clean RTA win takes months and requires documentation that most estimates are not built to support from the start.

Three of those four paths involve either luck or a significant fight. That is the honest reality of where the industry stands today.

The Math Nobody Actually Does

Here is where the conversation needs to get real. The gap between what most shops need to charge and what insurance offers is not $40 or $50 per hour. For most shops the real-world gap is $10 to $20 per billable labor hour — sometimes more, but that range is where the majority of shops live.

Run that math on a real repair. A moderate collision job — 20 billable hours of body labor. At a $15/hr gap that is $300 in rate difference on that repair order. At $20/hr it is $400. Most shops I talk to are somewhere in that range. You probably are too, even if you haven't sat down and done you math on it lately.

Now run the other side of that math. Your estimator spends time writing the initial estimate, then writes a supplement when the adjuster kicks back the rate, then makes phone calls, then documents the position, then follows up again. Conservative estimate: three to four hours of estimator time on a rate dispute for a single RO. At what that estimator costs your shop per hour — salary, benefits, overhead — you are spending real money to recover $300 to $400. The net gain shrinks fast. On some ROs it disappears entirely.

There is an old saying about stepping over dollars to pick up pennies. Shops that spend hours fighting a $300 labor rate difference while leaving at least $1,978 in documented, payable operations off the estimate are doing exactly that — in work boots, on a shop floor, with grease on their hands, stepping over a pile of cash to argue about the change on the ground. The pennies are real. The dollars are realer.

And that math only works if you win. Which, as we established above, is not guaranteed.

What Is Actually Happening While Your Estimator Fights That Battle

Picture your shop at 10am on a Tuesday. Your estimator is on the phone with an adjuster for the third time this week on the same RO, making the case for a labor rate the adjuster has already rejected twice.

While that call is happening, here is what else is happening:

The phone is ringing with a new customer who wants an estimate. That customer will wait, or they will call the shop down the street. The client whose car has been in the shop for a week has not received an update and is about to call their insurance company instead of you. A tech just walked in from the shop floor — the estimate missed a part, a procedure was not flagged, and now there is a conversation about who is going to eat the cost. Another tech is behind you saying he has not been paid for three hours on the last job and wants to know why. That obvious total loss that rolled in yesterday still has not been written because there is no time, and the insurance company is already calling it a bumper job.

The rate dispute on the first RO is costing your shop on every other RO in the building. The opportunity cost of the rate fight is invisible on paper and devastating in practice. I have lived every single one of those interruptions personally. Some days it felt like the shop was running me instead of the other way around.

The Long Game — Rate Suppression Is a Systemic Problem

None of this means the labor rate fight does not matter. It does. Rate suppression is real, it is documented, and it is one of the most significant financial pressures on independent collision shops in the country. Insurance carriers have spent decades using DRP agreements, steering, and market surveys to hold rates below what the free market would set — and below what shops need to operate sustainably.

But one shop winning a $300 rate argument on one RO does not move that needle. What moves it is data, documentation, and volume.

The Aequus Claims Door Rate Calculator is building exactly that. Every shop that submits their true calculated door rate — based on their real costs, not what insurance offers — adds to a national database of what collision repair actually costs. That data supports legislative testimony. It supports RTA filings. It supports the industry advocacy organizations working to change the regulatory framework that allows rate suppression to continue.

The fight for fair labor rates is worth having. It just needs to be fought at the right level — with the right tools, the right documentation, and the right data behind it.
